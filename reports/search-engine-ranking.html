How does a search engine work?

A search engine works in 3 stages, crawling, index and rank. The first step, crawling, is done prior to a search happening and works as a process of discovery using robots. (also known as spiders…shudders) These robots discover content by following links. The way it works is the crawl-bots discover a URL and then crawl through the site following it’s navigation and then following links to other pages and branching out from there to find yet more sites in an exponential process.

The links create a path and these results are stored in the search engine index using algorithms (these are a collection of formulas and processes that determine how the information is stored. The search engine index is a database containing all the discovered content that is deemed by the crawlers to be search worthy. This content is then ranked according to a number of factors, including the content of the site itself, links leading to the site (this is kind of like word of mouth or referrals) as well as the internal navigation of the site.

Why do different search-engines give different ranking to websites for the same search phrase?
Depending on the search engine, different priorities are placed on elements of what the page contains. For example, Google is very text driven with it’s rankings. Versus Bing, which may place more of a priority on images representing the search term. They also deal with keywords, differently, either by direct matches or by context, meaning the content behind the keyword. Meta keywords are also used with the exception of google, who has stated they will not use them. On top of this, backlinks, or links from other websites to the one being indexed, are dealt with differently. Google (the most used search engine by far which is why it keeps ending up being my example) values the quantity of the back link, meaning how reputable the site is that provided it. (basically how good is the referral) Other search engines such as Bing use both quality of back links and also quantity, so the more backlinks the higher the ranking. curatti.com seo differences between google and other search engines
What is a <meta /> tag?
Meta is described as data (information) about data. (according to w3schools.com) Meta is snippits of content within the page used to alert search engines. The meta tags are like flags to the search engine as to what the page is about. Keyword meta tags are exactly as they sound, keywords placed in the meta that are used to attract search engines. The idea with keyword meta tags is that they should be able to be read and give a general idea of what the site is about Due to over-use of keyword meta tags, Google no longer uses them as a part of its ranking algorithm because marketers found they could “over-stuff” their site with meta keywords of whatever was trending. Description meta tags are much more useful because they are used to explain what the site is about or describe it briefly, in fact it cant be too long because search engines only read them and only do so briefly. Description meta tags are a summary of the site. These are also visible to the searcher through the search results which could lure traffic based on the description. wordstream.com meta-tags
What is the purpose for Keyword and Description meta tags?
The main purpose is to get the page they are being used on raked higher in the search engine results.
Why might you include meta tags in your pages?
A description tag in my page would help the person reading their search results to know that my site is the link they are looking for. A seach for similar terms or shortened (for example googling "town fire" instead of "town fire department") will have different results and while the site may be included in the results of the search, the description tag as well as a keyword of "town" or "town fire" and/or "town fire department" will help the user recognize the desired link.
Do meta tags make your pages more easily searchable or improve its ranking?
While meta tags improve the users search experienece and get "clicks", (click through rates is a term I've come across, meaning people who click on the site), according to Google, they do not actually impact search engine rankings. so they make the site more easily searchable but do not improve it's ranking.
